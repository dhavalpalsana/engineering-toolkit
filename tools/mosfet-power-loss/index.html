Operating Parameters
Bus Voltage Vbus
The DC input voltage applied across the drain-source of the high-side switch in buck/half-bridge/full-bridge topologies. It is the voltage present when switching transitions occur.
Load Current Iload
The switching output/phase current. Used to calculate conduction losses (RMS current approximation) and switching crossover power losses.
Switching Frequency fsw
The rate at which the MOSFET is toggled on and off per second. Expressed in kilohertz (kHz). Switching losses, gate losses, reverse recovery losses, and output capacitive losses scale linearly with frequency.
Gate Drive Voltage Vgate
The voltage supplied by the gate driver to charge the MOSFET gate to its fully on-state. The magnitude directly influences gate loss and determines the actual RDS(on) obtained (consult datasheet curves).
Dead Time tdead
The blanking time inserted between complementary switches in half-bridge configurations to prevent shoot-through. During this interval, current conducts through the MOSFET body diode.
Duty Cycle D
The percentage of the switching period during which the target MOSFET is conducting in the on-state. Conduction losses scale proportionally with duty cycle.
MOSFET Specific Parameters (from Datasheet)
On-Resistance RDS(on)
The drain-source resistance when the MOSFET is fully turned on. Specified at a specific gate drive voltage (Vgate) and temperature. Lower values reduce conduction loss.
Total Gate Charge Qg
The total amount of charge (in nanocoulombs, nC) that must be injected into the gate terminal to fully turn on the MOSFET at the specified gate drive voltage. Lower Qg reduces gate drive loss.
Rise Time Tr
The time required for the drain current to rise to its full load value during turn-on. It represents the overlap period where high voltage and high current exist simultaneously, causing switching loss.
Fall Time Tf
The time required for the drain current to fall to zero during turn-off. Similar to rise time, it is the primary driver of turn-off switching loss.
Delay Times td(on) / td(off)
Turn-on and Turn-off delay times represent the time elapsed from the application of gate signal to the start of the switching transition. Though they delay the transition, they do not directly dissipate crossover power.
Reverse Recovery Charge Qrr
The charge accumulated in the body diode PN-junction during forward conduction that must be swept out when the diode is reverse-biased. Higher Qrr increases reverse recovery loss in hard-switching topologies.
Diode Voltage Vsd
The forward voltage drop of the internal body diode when conducting. Conduction occurs during the dead-time interval. Lower Vsd reduces dead-time conduction loss.
Output Capacitance Coss
The parasitic capacitance between the drain and source terminals. As the MOSFET switches, this capacitor is charged to Vbus and then fully discharged internally through the channel during turn-on, dissipating energy.

Conduction Loss Pcond

Pcond = Iload² × RDS(on) × (D / 100)

Conduction loss occurs due to the internal resistive voltage drop across the drain-source channel when the MOSFET is fully enhanced and carrying load current.

Iload: Load current (A)
RDS(on): Drain-source on-resistance (Ω) - *convert from mΩ by dividing by 1000*
D: Conduction Duty Cycle (%) - *divided by 100*

Design Recommendation

  • Select a MOSFET with a lower RDS(on). Note that RDS(on) increases significantly with temperature (up to 1.5x–2.0x at 125°C).
  • Provide adequate heatsinking to keep junction temperatures low, preventing thermal runaway and rise in RDS(on).

Switching Loss Psw

Psw = ½ × Vbus × Iload × (Tr + Tf) × fsw

Switching loss is caused by the temporary overlap of drain-source voltage and drain current during the turn-on (rise time Tr) and turn-off (fall time Tf) intervals. During these intervals, the instantaneous power dissipated in the channel spikes dramatically.

Vbus: Bus Voltage (V)
Iload: Load current (A)
Tr / Tf: Rise and Fall times (s) - *convert from ns by dividing by 1e9*
fsw: Switching frequency (Hz) - *convert from kHz by multiplying by 1000*

Design Recommendation

  • Use a stronger gate driver to source/sink higher current to charge/discharge the gate faster, reducing Tr and Tf.
  • Decrease switching frequency fsw if switching losses dominate conduction losses.
  • Choose devices with lower gate-drain charge (Crss / Qgd) to speed up transition periods.

Dead Time Conduction Loss Pdead

Pdead = 2 × Iload × Vsd × tdead × fsw

In bridge topologies, dead time is required to prevent shoot-through currents. During dead time, both the high-side and low-side MOSFETs are off. To maintain current continuity, the load current forces the internal body diode to conduct, which has a higher voltage drop (Vsd ≈ 0.7V–1.2V) than the enhanced channel, leading to conduction losses.

Iload: Load current (A)
Vsd: Diode forward drop (V)
tdead: Dead time (s) - *convert from ns by dividing by 1e9*
fsw: Switching frequency (Hz) - *convert from kHz by multiplying by 1000*

Design Recommendation

  • Minimize dead time tdead to the minimum safe duration that prevents shoot-through (dynamic gate-sensing or precise microcontroller timers).
  • Add an external Schottky diode in parallel with the MOSFET. Schottky diodes have a lower forward drop (Vsd ≈ 0.4V) and zero reverse recovery time.

Body Diode Reverse Recovery Loss Prr

Prr = Qrr × Vbus × fsw

When the body diode conducts during dead time, a charge Qrr is stored in its PN junction. When the complementary switch turns on, a large reverse current flows to sweep out this charge under the full Vbus voltage. This sweeps energy out, dissipating power primarily in the recovering diode and turning-on switch.

Qrr: Reverse recovery charge (C) - *convert from nC by dividing by 1e9*
Vbus: Bus voltage (V)
fsw: Switching frequency (Hz) - *convert from kHz by multiplying by 1000*

Design Recommendation

  • Select devices marketed with "Fast Recovery" or specialized body diodes (like Infineon's OptiMOS with integrated Schottky-like diodes).
  • Use Wide-Bandgap semiconductors (GaN or SiC) which exhibit very low or zero reverse recovery charges.

Gate Drive Loss Pgate

Pgate = Qg × Vgate × fsw

To turn the MOSFET on, the gate-source capacitance must be charged to Vgate. To turn it off, this charge must be removed. The power required is dissipated across the entire gate loop (including the gate driver internal resistance, external gate resistor, and internal gate mesh resistance).

Qg: Total gate charge (C) - *convert from nC by dividing by 1e9*
Vgate: Gate drive voltage (V)
fsw: Switching frequency (Hz) - *convert from kHz by multiplying by 1000*

Design Recommendation

  • Use a logic-level MOSFET or lower gate drive voltage Vgate if the RDS(on) penalty is negligible.
  • Select a MOSFET with a lower gate charge Qg for high frequency operation.

Output Capacitance Loss Pcoss

Pcoss = ½ × Coss × Vbus² × fsw

The drain-source output capacitance Coss is charged to Vbus when the MOSFET is off. When the MOSFET turns on, this electrostatic energy is short-circuited through the channel and dissipated entirely as heat inside the silicon device.

Coss: Output capacitance (F) - *convert from pF by dividing by 1e12*
Vbus: Bus voltage (V)
fsw: Switching frequency (Hz) - *convert from kHz by multiplying by 1000*

Design Recommendation

  • For soft-switching topologies (like LLC, phase-shifted full bridge), Coss energy can be recycled into the tank circuit rather than dissipated.
  • Select devices with lower parasitic output capacitance Coss.
